Privilege Escalation Response Runbook

Summary
Privilege escalation occurs when an attacker gains rights beyond those initially granted, moving from a standard user to root/administrator or to higher-privileged service accounts. Vectors include kernel exploits, misconfigured sudo, SUID binaries, weak file permissions, token theft, and abuse of cloud IAM roles. Escalation typically precedes persistence and lateral movement.

Detection
1. Alert on unexpected use of privileged commands: sudo to root by unusual users, su attempts, creation of new admin/sudoers entries, and changes to /etc/sudoers or local Administrators group.
2. Watch for execution of known exploit tooling, abnormal SUID/SETUID binary use, and processes spawning shells with elevated tokens.
3. In cloud, alert on IAM policy changes, AssumeRole anomalies, and attachment of high-privilege policies to users or roles.

Containment
1. Suspend the implicated account(s) and revoke active sessions and tokens; in cloud, detach the abused role/policy.
2. Isolate the affected host via EDR or network quarantine to prevent lateral movement.
3. Block any attacker-controlled persistence (cron jobs, systemd units, scheduled tasks, new SSH keys).

Eradication and Remediation
1. Patch the exploited vulnerability (kernel, service, or misconfiguration) and remove unnecessary SUID bits.
2. Enforce least privilege: tighten sudoers, remove standing admin rights, and adopt just-in-time elevation.
3. Rotate credentials and secrets accessible from the compromised context, including service accounts and cloud keys.

Recovery and Post-Incident
1. Rebuild compromised hosts from trusted images where root access was achieved.
2. Review audit logs to scope the full extent of access and identify any data accessed.
3. Add detections for the specific escalation technique (mapped to MITRE ATT&CK) and validate with a follow-up access review.
